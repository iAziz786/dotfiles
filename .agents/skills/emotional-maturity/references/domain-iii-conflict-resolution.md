# Domain III: Conflict Resolution (Traits 13-19)

Detailed examples for conflict resolution skills.

## 13. Repair Capacity

**Uses humor, affection, apology, or de-escalation during conflict**
- EM and partner are arguing about chores, EM makes a silly face, both laugh, tension breaks, they resume conversation more calmly
- Mid-fight, EM reaches out and touches partner's hand, says "I don't want to fight with you. I want to understand you"
- After raising voice, EM stops, takes breath, says "I'm sorry I yelled. That wasn't okay. Can we start over?"

**Recognizes when to pause, offer repair, or accept partner's repair**
- EM notices they're flooded (heart racing, can't think), says "I need 20 minutes. I promise I'll come back to this. I love you"
- Partner offers a repair attempt ("Can we take a break?"), EM accepts even though they want to keep arguing: "Yes, good idea. Let's pause"
- EM sees partner is trying to de-escalate with humor, resists the urge to stay angry, allows the repair to land

**Makes multiple repair attempts per conflict**
- First repair: "I don't want us to go to bed angry"
- Second repair: "I can see you're hurt. That wasn't my intention"
- Third repair: "Can I hold you for a minute before we figure this out?"

## 14. Maintains the 5:1 Ratio

**Maintains 5 positive interactions per 1 negative during conflict**
- During argument about parenting styles: EM validates partner's concern ("You're right, safety is important"), shares a memory of partner being a great parent, thanks them for bringing it up, asks about their childhood, ends with "I know we both want what's best"—that's 5 positives around 1 disagreement
- EM criticizes partner for being late, but also: appreciates they texted to say they'd be late, mentions a time partner was thoughtful about time, expresses confidence in their growth, asks if something else is going on, and makes a joke about "CP time"—5 positives cushioning 1 complaint
- In discussion about finances, EM: acknowledges partner's hard work, expresses gratitude for transparency, validates anxiety about money, shares hope about their future, suggests a solution—and then raises the concern about overspending

**Uses validation, soft start-ups, appreciative comments amidst disagreement**
- "I know you're trying your best, and I also need to talk about..."
- "I love how generous you are with our friends, and I'm worried about..."
- "You were so patient with the kids today. I want to discuss something that's been bothering me..."

## 15. Non-Defensive Listening

**Paraphrases before responding, asks clarifying questions**
- Partner: "You never help around the house!" EM: "It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed and alone in the responsibilities. Is that right? Tell me more."
- Partner criticizes EM's parenting, EM: "So you're concerned I'm too permissive. Can you give me a specific example of when you felt that way?"
- Partner expresses hurt, EM: "If I'm hearing you right, you felt dismissed when I said X. Did I get that?"

**Practices physiological self-soothing**
- EM feels defensive, notices heart racing, asks for water, takes sip, slows breathing to <100 BPM before responding
- During intense conversation, EM takes bathroom break, does 2 minutes of box breathing, returns regulated
- EM notices jaw clenching, consciously relaxes it, drops shoulders, grounds feet before continuing

**Takes 20-minute breaks when flooded, returns to discussion**
- EM says "I'm overwhelmed. I need 20 minutes. I'll set a timer and come back at 3:30"
- During break, EM journals, walks, or meditates—does NOT stew or plan rebuttals
- EM returns exactly when promised, initiates: "Okay, I'm ready to listen better now"

## 16. Taking Responsibility Without Over-Responsibility

**Accepts partner's influence 80%+ of the time**
- Partner suggests different vacation plan, EM considers it seriously, often says "That's actually a better idea. Let's do that"
- Partner expresses preference for restaurant, EM: "I wanted Italian, but you're more invested in this choice. Let's go where you want"
- Partner shares perspective on conflict, EM: "I hadn't thought of it that way. You're right, I did..." and adjusts behavior

**Owns specific behavior without global self-criticism**
- "I was defensive when you brought that up. That was my stuff. I'm sorry"—not "I'm a terrible partner"
- "I forgot our anniversary. That's on me. Here's how I'll prevent it in future"—not "I'm the worst, you should leave me"
- "I raised my voice. That wasn't okay. I was frustrated about work and took it out on you"—specific, contextual, repair-focused

**Distinguishes "I contributed to this problem" from "I'm a terrible person"**
- EM can say "Yes, I played a role in this conflict" without collapsing into shame spiral
- EM acknowledges "I haven't been initiating intimacy" without concluding "I'm fundamentally unlovable"
- EM admits "I was dismissive of your feelings" while maintaining "I'm still a good person who made a mistake"

## 17. Softened Start-Up

**Communicates directly: "I feel [emotion] about [specific situation]"**
- "I feel lonely when we don't have dinner together. Can we prioritize that?"
- "I'm worried about our savings. Can we look at the budget together?"
- "I felt hurt when you joked about my cooking in front of your parents"

**Avoids criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling**
- Doesn't say "You always/never..." (criticism)
- Doesn't roll eyes, mock, or use sarcasm (contempt)
- Doesn't say "Yeah but you..." when receiving feedback (defensiveness)
- Doesn't walk out or give silent treatment (stonewalling)

## 18. Agreeing to Disagree

**Recognizes 69% of conflicts are perpetual (not solvable)**
- EM and partner fundamentally differ on tidiness standards. EM: "This is our perpetual issue. You're neater, I'm messier. How do we manage this difference rather than solve it?"
- EM wants kids, partner doesn't. EM: "This is a dealbreaker or something we need ongoing dialogue about. It's not a problem to be fixed."
- EM and partner have different political views. EM: "We see the world differently. Can we create space for both truths in our relationship?"

**Moves from "Who's right?" to "How do we manage this difference?"**
- Different parenting styles: not "Who's the better parent?" but "How do we blend our approaches?"
- Different risk tolerance with money: not "Who's smarter about investing?" but "How do we balance security and growth?"
- Different social needs: not "Who's the normal one?" but "How much alone time vs. together time do we each need?"

**Acknowledges partner's validity while maintaining position**
- "I understand why you see it that way. Your perspective makes sense. I still see it differently, and that's okay"
- "You're right that my way has risks. Your way has different risks. Neither is wrong, just different"
- "We both have valid needs here. Yours for X, mine for Y. How do we honor both?"

## 19. Conflict as Information

**Views disagreement as data about needs and values**
- EM notices recurring conflict about time together and realizes: "This is telling me quality time is my love language. Let me communicate that clearly"
- EM sees they fight most about money when stressed, understands: "Money represents security for me. When I'm anxious, I need more reassurance about finances"
- EM recognizes conflict about parenting reveals: "I need to feel like a competent parent. Criticism of my parenting hits my core insecurity"

**Asks "What does this tell us?" rather than "How do we win?"**
- "We're arguing about the same thing again. What is this pattern trying to show us about our needs?"
- "This fight happened right after your promotion. What might that timing tell us?"
- "We both feel misunderstood. What do we need to change about how we communicate?"
